Web Frontend Development II

WDD 330

Instructor Preparation

Course Outline

This course attempts to simulate working on a real project in a real frontend team. The first 4.5 weeks will be spent adding features and fixing bugs on a project called SleepOutside, a simple outdoor gear retail site.

Team/Individual Activities

During most weeks the students will have an individual activity which they will complete on their own, as well as a team activity which they should complete in a peer programming style with their team. Following instructions in the W01 Icebreaker and Team Enrollment, students will self-enroll in a group (ideally 3-4 students each) for team activities throughout the semester. At midweek of W01, check to ensure that each student is in a group and that each group has between 3-4 students.

Most of the points for the team activities come from participation: only about 10% or so is for completion.

Make sure to stress to them that peer programming means that one student drives...they are on the editor typing code. The rest of the team tells them what to type. Most of the input should come from those NOT typing.

The team/individual activities are used to make major changes to the application. The students will also be given a Trello board full of cards that fix bugs and add smaller bits of functionality. Teams should have the goal of completing at least one card per person in the team per week. Students should attempt the cards individually, but should feel welcome to reach out to their teammates for help as needed.

A typical week should have the following:

  1. Reading assignments: ~2hrs
  2. Team & individual activities: ~4-6hrs
  3. Trello cards: ~3hrs
  4. Professor led instruction: ~1hr (recorded weekly office hour)

Final Project

Part two is weeks 5-7 and will be spent building the frontend of a new application that the teams have planned earlier in the course. There will still be readings, but no more team activities.

Instructor Time Expectations

The course is designed to minimize the amount of time you spend grading to allow for more time teaching and giving individualized attention to students that need it. Each week the students are required to turn in either notes or worked exercises. You should review those with the goal of extracting questions to address with the class. Encourage your students to include their questions in their notes!

They will submit a weekly SCRUM-style report of status in I-Learn. These reports are your best tool for finding students whom you may need to reach out to. Grade this activity on completion. Feel free to dock a few points on the notes if you feel they are putting in minimal effort.

You should plan on holding an office hour each week. Record this for those that can't make it. Use this time to answer any questions you find in the notes or that the students attending bring, and then work on a coding example illustrating skills from the readings. Each week in the Teaching Notes I'll give an example project...use mine or feel free to come up with your own.

You may also consider preparing a short video introduction for each week to help the students plan their time.

Final Grades

The grading for the course is a bit different. 30% of the grade will be made up of the weekly notes and team/individual activities. The other 70% will be based off of a self-evaluation that the student performs. They will assess themselves on how well they have achieved the outcomes of the course, prepare a short video with their justifications, and then provide a portfolio of work to back up their claims. You should review their marks and their submitted work and adjust as necessary. A major part of that portfolio should be their final team project.
